Philosophy

How I look at life — my own words.

Existential since conscious. For years, I've dreaded the fact that everything will end eventually. This idea is a fundamental part of life — nothing would make any sense if things did not have an end to them. Still, it's a feeling I wouldn't wish on my worst enemies; probably one of the worst emotional sensations a human can feel.

When I got into university, this view changed when I started diving into philosophy in my humanities course. We examined deep fundamental ideas and problems related to the human being — individual-social relationships, religion, culture, and more. I started seeing that there is some sort of immortality and deep meaning in human existence.

This went on to our philosophy courses, starting from the ancients (Plato, Aristotle, Cicero), then the medieval religious authors (Saint Augustine, Saint Thomas Aquinas). I'm a fan of Aristotle and Saint Augustine, even though I'm not Catholic — really interesting ideas that get completely demolished by the moderns. My favorite philosopher is Hume, who talks about empiricism and the absurdity of causality in such a rational and convincing way. Kant is a pretty great synthesis of all of it.

Religion

My religious perspective is agnostic with some spiritualism involved. I firmly believe that God is an inaccessible concept to the human mind — the God we get to think about is just human faculties taken to infinity. Still, I have faith that the order of the universe, human nature, consciousness, rationality — none of these are just a coincidence.

Death is a meta-human experience that goes beyond whatever can fit into our comprehension. We will transition from the rules of one world to another where human concepts don't apply. Death is incomprehensible for our mind — but we can notice that across virtually every culture, throughout history, there have been spiritualist practices pointing toward that intersection. Whatever happens after death, it will be something. A transition into a new world, not just end and emptiness.

Society

Society falls into the big lie of neoliberalism: that there is freedom in the market and equal opportunity for everyone who wants to participate. This is not true, and it is incredibly easy to find counter-examples — millionaires who live in luxury without moving a finger because they inherited their wealth, or poor people who work extremely hard their entire lives and never get anywhere socioeconomically. You have definitely seen at least one of these in your personal life.

This gets denied with statistics too. One quick dive into the World Inequality Lab, and you'll find shocking numbers: the bottom 50% of the population holds only 2% of global wealth, while the top 10% holds 75% — that is 37.5 times more (WIL, 2025).

We need to urgently address this economic discrepancy. Millionaires and cultural background have morphed our mentalities to accept and even promote these inequalities, and that is unacceptable. Making consciousness about it is the least I can do for now — but it is one of my goals to take this task more seriously in the future.
